RL-MG: Reinforcement Learning Framework for Magnetic Guidewire
Path Following
Jun Luo1,2, Zhiqiang Chen 1,2, Mingxue Cai 2, Shunyuan Huang 2, Shixiong Fu 2,
Dong Li2, Chenyang Huang 2, Tiantian Xu 2
Abstract— Magnetic guidewires (MG), featuring active steer-
ing capabilities, show great potential for enhancing inter-
ventional surgery due to their ﬂexibility and controllability.
However, achieving real-time autonomous navigation of a MG
within complex vascular networks remains a major challenge.
This study presents a reinforcement learning (RL)-based control
framework for MG path following in vascular navigation tasks.
The tip of the MG is equipped with an embedded small
permanent magnet, enabling magnetic actuation via a movable
external magnet (EM). The proposed RL framework is trained
in a Simulation Open Framework Architecture (SOFA)-based
simulation, where it learns to control the motion of the EM and
the translation of the MG to accurately follow a predeﬁned
reference path. To validate the effectiveness of the trained
RL policy, an experimental setup is established, comprising an
MG prototype, an EM mounted on a robotic arm, a vascular
phantom model, and a depth camera for visual feedback.
Experimental results demonstrate that the RL policy achieves
reliable path following performance in physical environments,
with an overall mean tracking error of 3.39 ± 1.87 mm (mean
± SD) across all four tested paths.
I. I NTRODUCTION
Small-scale soft continuum systems can achieve remote-
controlled active steering and navigation via tendon-
driven [1], pneumatic [2], hydraulic [3], magnetic [4], or
hybrid actuation methods [5]. Among these, magnetic actu-
ation has attracted signiﬁcant attention due to its contactless
control, biocompatibility, and low mechanical complexity.
Magnetic continuum robots (MCRs) improve controllabil-
ity by incorporating magnetized tips—such as embedded
permanent magnets [6] or magnetic particles [7]—and are
actuated via externally applied magnetic ﬁelds, either from
permanent magnets or electromagnets. Various innovative
MCR designs have been developed, including submillimeter-
scale soft robots with ferromagnetic hydrogel skins [8],

Building upon these efforts, this paper proposes an RL-
based control framework for magnetic guidewire navigation.
The framework is implemented in a SOFA-based simulation
environment [24], where MG dynamics are modeled using
the BeamAdapter plugin [25], and magnetic actuation is
realized through external permanent magnets. The RL agent
learns to coordinate both the movement of the EM and the
guidewire trajectory to achieve high-precision path follow-
ing. The trained policy is validated through experiments
conducted in both simulation and on a real system. The
main contribution of this work is the development of a com-
plete and transferable RL-based framework that integrates
magnetic actuation, high-ﬁdelity simulation, and real-world
validation, providing a practical platform for applying and
benchmarking intelligent control strategies in magnetically
actuated vascular navigation.
The structure of this paper is organized as follows: Section
II presents the system design and modeling; Section III in-
troduces the reinforcement learning-based control framework
and training process; Section IV describes the experimental
setup and results; and Section V concludes the paper and
discusses future research directions.
II. S
YSTEM DESIGN AND MODELING
As shown in Fig. 1, the system consists of an external
magnet (EM), a vascular model, and a magnetic guidewire
(MG) with a small permanent magnet embedded at its tip.
The EM generates a magnetic ﬁeld that applies torque to
the MG tip, enabling steering. The vascular model mimics
the anatomical environment for guidewire navigation. By
controlling both the EM motion and the MG translation,
the system guides the MG along a predeﬁned reference
trajectory.
The MG’s mechanical behavior is simulated in the SOFA
framework [24] using the BeamAdapter plugin [25], based
on Kirchhoff rod theory. The guidewire is discretized into
n nodes using a one-dimensional ﬁnite element method.
Contact with the vascular wall is handled via the Lagrange
multiplier method [18].

ETHOD
In this section, we present the reinforcement learning con-
trol framework for the magnetic guidewire (RL-MG), using
RL methods to control MG to reach the target point along the
centerline of the vascular model, as shown in Fig. 3. The task
is formulated as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), where
the RL-MG agent interacts with the environment (vascular
model) and the EM through a sequence of states, actions,
and rewards to learn an optimal policy for effective path
tracking and navigation. We apply the Deep Deterministic
Policy Gradient (DDPG) algorithm, and also use several
regularization techniques to stabilize the training process. For
the real-world experiments, a depth camera and YOLOv11
[26] are used to localize the guidewire tip.
1) Environment: Training directly in the real world is
inefﬁcient and unsafe, especially with the EM mounted on
a robotic manipulator. Therefore, a simulation environment
based on SOFA [24] and BeamAdapter [25] is developed.

vascular model, an MG, and a cylindrical EM. The EM’s
magnetic ﬁeld is obtained via COMSOL, and users can
control the EM’s 6-DOF motion and MG insertion via a
keyboard interface. The trained agent is evaluated in both
simulation and real-world settings.

IV . EXPERIMENTS AND RESULTS
A. Experimental setup
The experiments are conducted in a full-scale vascular
model, as illustrated in Fig. 4. On the left side, a col-
laborative robot (Universal Robots UR5, Universal Robots
A/S, Odense, Denmark) is equipped with a 60 mm × 60
mm permanent magnet attached to its end-effector. On the
right side, a guidewire propulsion device is positioned, which
has been previously described in detail in earlier work
from our lab [4]. Inside the vascular model, a medical
guidewire ﬁtted with a small permanent magnet at its tip
serves as the controlled object. An Intel RealSense D455
depth camera (Intel Corporation, Santa Clara, CA, USA)
is mounted above the vascular model to capture depth data
during experiments. The reinforcement learning model was
trained on a workstation running Ubuntu 20.04, equipped
with an Intel® Core™ i9-13900K CPU and an NVIDIA RTX
4090 GPU. The GPU provided accelerated computation sup-
port during model training. The Robotic Operating System
(ROS, version Noetic) was used for system control and data
communication.

B. Dataset and Training Settings
In this study, the RL agent is trained in a SOFA-based
simulation environment using datasets comprising multiple
paths within vascular models, as shown in Fig. 5. To simulate
real blood vessels, we designed various vascular models in
SolidWorks, reserving a real femoral artery model for testing.
The experimental setup provides the EM with two degrees of
freedom: translation along the y-axis and rotation about the
z-axis. To avoid collisions with the water tank and vascular
structures, the magnet starts from either side of the tank.
Due to rapid magnetic ﬁeld attenuation on the opposite side
of the EM, insufﬁcient torque reaches the MG tip when the
EM is positioned unilaterally. Therefore, reference paths are
grouped into left-side and right-side categories.
We adopted standard training settings for the DDPG agent,
including a learning rate of 0.0001 for the policy network,
0.001 for the Q-value network, a discount factor of 0.99,
and trained for 400 episodes. Additionally, we employed the
optimizer proposed in [28] to improve convergence speed
and boost training efﬁciency.
C. Results
We verify the effectiveness and performance of our method
by deploying the best trained model on a real-world system.
Four paths in the vascular model are selected: two paths
oriented along the larger x-axis direction and two along the
smaller x-axis direction. Accordingly, the EM at the robot
arm’s end-effector is initialized at different positions. At
each time step, the states for RL are obtained from the UR5
robot and depth camera, while the actions are generated by
policy networks. These actions are sent to the UR5 and the
guidewire advancer via ROS, guiding the guidewire along
the selected reference path.

The experimental results, summarized in Table I, demon-
strate the effectiveness of our method in both simulated and
real-world environments. All tracking errors are reported
as mean ± standard deviation (SD) over three repeated
trials for each path. In simulation, the RL agent achieved
relatively small positional errors and short task completion
times. In the real-world experiments, although the agent
maintained comparable positional accuracy, the completion
time was notably longer. This discrepancy primarily arises
from limitations in the depth camera’s detection accuracy —
the MG tip occupies a very small number of pixels (20–30
pixels), which introduces localization errors in the detection.
Furthermore, inherent differences between the simulated and
real environments make it difﬁcult to ensure accurate state
estimation, which contributes to the increased task duration.
As illustrated in Fig. 6, the agent occasionally misinterpreted
the MG tip’s deviation from the reference path (e.g., path
2 from t = 0 s to t = 34 s, and path 4 from t = 11 s to
t = 45 s), resulting in excessive time spent correcting small
deviations. In these cases, the agent tended to slow down the
MG movement and adjust its position via EM manipulation
or rotation to stay close to the reference path.
It is worth noting that a direct comparison with tradi-
tional model-based control methods is not included in this
study. Such methods typically require accurate modeling of

In contrast, our RL-based approach learns control policies

V. C
ONCLUSION
This study presents an RL-based control framework for
magnetic guidewire path following, trained in a SOFA-based
simulation environment and validated through real-world
experiments. The RL agent achieved low positional errors
and short task completion times in simulation. In real-world
experiments, although the system was able to complete the
control tasks, the execution time increased due to perception
inaccuracies and domain discrepancies between simulation
and reality. Despite these challenges, our approach highlights
the potential of RL-based autonomous guidewire control. The
developed simulation environment offers a reliable platform
for validating alternative control strategies, and the fully
autonomous control method provides valuable insights for
robot-assisted vascular interventions.
In future work, we will explore the extension of the current
2D framework to 3D navigation tasks by increasing the
degrees of freedom of magnetic actuation (e.g., enabling
3D positioning and orientation control of the external mag-
net) and upgrading the state representation to include full
3D positional and directional information. Combined with
anatomically realistic 3D vascular models in simulation and
experiment, these improvements will enhance the frame-
work’s applicability to more clinically relevant scenarios.
